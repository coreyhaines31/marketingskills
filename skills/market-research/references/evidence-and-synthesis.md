# Evidence and Synthesis Guide

Use this reference when the market-research skill needs to turn incomplete signals into a decision-grade recommendation.

## Evidence hierarchy

Rank evidence by how close it is to buyer behavior.

1. **First-party commercial data**: sales notes, CRM data, conversion data, churn reasons, paid search terms, demo recordings, support tickets.
2. **Observed market behavior**: competitor ads, pricing pages, review volume, job posts, marketplace listings, directory rankings, SERP structure, public case studies.
3. **Search and demand proxies**: keyword volume, CPC, related queries, autocomplete, category search patterns, social listening.
4. **Third-party reports**: analyst reports, industry reports, census or government data, platform reports, investor reports.
5. **Expert opinion**: founder interviews, practitioner posts, podcasts, newsletters, conference talks.
6. **Weak proxies**: vanity engagement, generic trend articles, uncited market-size claims, AI-generated summaries.

Prefer direct buyer and competitor behavior over broad market-size claims.

## Confidence rules

Use a confidence label for each major claim.

### High confidence

Use when at least two independent evidence types point to the same conclusion.

Example:

- Search demand is visible
- Competitors are actively buying ads
- Reviews show repeated pain
- Multiple players have pricing pages or expansion signals

### Medium confidence

Use when evidence is credible but incomplete.

Example:

- Competitors exist and are active, but spend levels are unclear
- Demand is visible in social conversations, but search intent is weak
- Reports suggest growth, but local market evidence is limited

### Low confidence

Use when the conclusion is plausible but weakly supported.

Example:

- A category is trending globally, but local adoption is unknown
- A segment looks underserved, but willingness to pay is not proven
- A channel appears promising, but CAC cannot be estimated yet

### Unknown

Use when the information is missing, contradictory, or outside the available research scope.

Do not turn unknowns into claims. Turn them into validation tasks.

## Fact, interpretation, implication

Separate every important point into three layers:

| Layer | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Fact | What the evidence directly shows | Three local competitors run Google Search ads for emergency dental services |
| Interpretation | What that probably means | There is active high-intent demand and paid competition |
| Implication | What the user should do | Start with narrower treatment keywords and stronger trust proof instead of broad clinic terms |

This prevents the research from sounding more certain than it is.

## Triangulation patterns

Use triangulation when a clean market-size number is unavailable.

### Bottom-up sizing

Use when you can estimate units.

Formula:

`reachable buyers × expected annual spend × realistic capture rate`

Good for local services, clinics, agencies, B2B niches, marketplaces, and service categories.

### Search-led sizing

Use when active demand is visible.

Estimate from:

- High-intent keyword clusters
- Local modifiers
- CPC levels
- Competitor ad density
- Landing page count
- Conversion assumptions

Do not equate search volume with market size. Search volume is an intent proxy.

### Competitor-led sizing

Use when competitors reveal market maturity.

Estimate from:

- Number of direct competitors
- Locations, headcount, funding, or client count
- Visible pricing
- Review volume and recency
- Content footprint
- Paid media activity

### Channel-led sizing

Use when the user wants a GTM recommendation.

Estimate from:

- Reachable audience by channel
- Cost to test
- Expected qualified response rate
- Funnel conversion constraints
- Sales capacity

This is often more useful than TAM for early-stage decisions.

## Market attractiveness scorecard

Score each factor 1-5.

| Factor | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Demand clarity | Pain is unclear | Pain exists but intent is mixed | Clear repeated buying/search behavior |
| Budget availability | No clear payer | Payer exists but value is uncertain | Clear budget owner and spend precedent |
| Competitive weakness | Strong incumbents everywhere | Some gaps exist | Obvious gaps buyers care about |
| Reachability | Hard to target | Reachable with work | Easy to reach through known channels |
| Channel feasibility | Channels are expensive or unclear | Some testable channels | Clear low-risk validation path |
| Urgency | Nice-to-have | Periodic need | Triggered, painful, time-sensitive need |
| Proof availability | Hard to prove | Some proof can be built | Strong proof is easy to show |
| Risk | Heavy regulatory or operational risk | Manageable constraints | Low execution risk |
| Speed to signal | Months to learn | 2-6 weeks | 7-14 days |
| Strategic fit | Weak fit | Partial fit | Strong fit with capabilities and assets |

Interpretation:

- **40-50**: Attractive market. Recommend entry or focused expansion.
- **30-39**: Viable but needs a narrow wedge.
- **20-29**: Validate before committing budget.
- **Below 20**: Avoid unless the user has a unique advantage.

## Common research mistakes

Avoid these failure modes:

- Treating TAM as proof of demand
- Confusing social hype with buyer intent
- Overweighting global reports for local-market decisions
- Listing competitors without explaining what they mean
- Calling a gap "white space" without proving customers care
- Ignoring substitutes and status quo
- Recommending channels without considering budget, creative burden, and measurement quality
- Presenting precise numbers when the data only supports a range
- Ending with insights but no validation plan

## Final synthesis rule

Every market research output should end with one of four decisions:

1. **Enter now**
2. **Enter narrowly**
3. **Validate first**
4. **Avoid for now**

Then give the next 3-5 actions required to reduce uncertainty or move into execution.
